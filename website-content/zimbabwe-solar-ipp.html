Zimbabwe Solar Market Feasibility

IPP Opportunity Analysis: Turning Crisis into Capacity

Zimbabwe faces a critical energy deficit of up to 1,000MW daily. With average solar irradiation of 20MJ/m²/day, the country presents a prime opportunity for Independent Power Producers (IPPs). This report analyzes the feasibility of entering the market.

1. The Energy Crisis: Supply vs. Demand

The fundamental driver for any PPA model in Zimbabwe is the chronic supply deficit. Reliance on aging thermal plants (Hwange) and drought-sensitive hydro (Kariba) has created a volatile grid.

Current Generation Mix Reliability

Hydro dependency poses climate risk, while thermal suffers from frequent breakdowns. Solar currently constitutes a minor fraction, highlighting the growth gap.

The Deficit Gap (Peak Demand)

Local generation consistently falls short of the ~1,800MW to 2,200MW peak demand, forcing expensive imports or load shedding.

4. Financial Feasibility & Tariffs

ZESA tariffs have historically lagged behind cost-reflectivity ($0.10 - $0.12/kWh). IPPs require a tariff that covers debt service, usually indexed to USD.

Feasibility Verdict: Proceed with Caution

Strengths & Opportunities

  • ✔ Excellent solar irradiation ensures high technical yield.
  • ✔ Critical national power shortage guarantees demand.
  • ✔ Government incentives (National Project Status) available.
  • ✔ Opportunity for Private PPAs (Mines/Industry) to bypass ZESA credit risk.

Risks & Threats

  • ⚠ Currency convertibility (ZWG to USD) for profit repatriation.
  • ⚠ ZETDC (Offtaker) creditworthiness issues.
  • ⚠ Grid stability issues affecting power evacuation.

Strategic Recommendation

Starting a solar company is feasible IF the business model targets Commercial & Industrial (C&I) clients (Mines, Factories) for direct PPAs, rather than relying solely on the national utility. This mitigates currency risk and payment default risk.
